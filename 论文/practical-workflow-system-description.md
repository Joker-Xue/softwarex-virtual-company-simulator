# Practical Workflow and System Description

The system is designed for students, participants in career-planning courses, and early-stage learners who need a concrete way to understand different workplace roles. It provides an interactive, observable, and explainable virtual company environment that turns abstract career descriptions into a simulated workplace process. A user first creates or selects a virtual role profile by specifying a nickname, target department, MBTI type, and six role attributes: communication, leadership, creativity, technical ability, teamwork, and diligence. The user then enters a three-floor virtual company. The first floor contains the lobby, cafe, and HR department; the second floor contains business departments such as engineering, product, marketing, finance, and operations; and the third floor contains the meeting room, director office, and CEO office. The company is populated with NPC agents that cover different MBTI types, departments, career levels, and management or technical growth paths. These NPCs represent roles across engineering, product, marketing, finance, HR, operations, and management, allowing students to compare how different personality-role combinations behave in the same organizational setting.

During the simulation, the user explores the company as a first-person career learner and observes how NPC agents respond to daily tasks, meetings, breaks, social interactions, and dynamic company events. A background simulation loop periodically drives agent behavior. Each NPC chooses among actions such as working, chatting, resting, attending meetings, and moving through the workplace according to its MBTI preference, current task queue, daily schedule, department location, career level, and event interest. For example, introverted and judging-oriented roles tend to show higher work focus and task efficiency, while extraverted or feeling-oriented roles are more active in social interaction and relationship growth. Intuitive roles receive stronger benefits in complex or creative tasks, thinking-oriented roles tend to move toward a technical career path, and feeling-oriented roles tend to move toward a management path. Different departments also generate role-specific tasks: engineering roles may refactor API endpoints, write unit tests, or tune performance; product roles may review requirements or write PRDs; marketing roles may plan campaigns or analyze user data; finance roles may review budgets or prepare risk reports; and HR roles may screen resumes, conduct interviews, or design onboarding plans. As a result, the user does not only read what a role is, but observes its task structure, interaction partners, workplace location, and growth process across virtual workdays.

The system outputs both the visual simulation state and multiple layers of explainable diagnostic information. In the role panel, users can inspect career level, XP, task completion, friendships, chat records, company events, and analytics. The diagnostic interface further shows how personality parameters influence behavior, including work-speed multiplier, social-gain multiplier, XP multiplier, career-path tendency, action probability distribution, preferred task tags, event participation tendency, and recent decision timeline. Each task selection, task completion, social interaction, and event participation is recorded as a behavior trace, including preference scores, matching reasons, XP rewards, relationship changes, and location changes. The system also provides personal and company-level dashboards, such as personal XP growth, task status distribution, friend affinity, department population, average XP by department, and career-level distribution. These outputs allow users to understand career experience from both a process-observation perspective and a data-explanation perspective.

In practical use, the system can serve as a classroom demonstration tool for career-planning courses, a small-scale internal trial platform, or a workshop activity for career exploration. An instructor can ask students to choose different MBTI types and departments, enter the same virtual company for several virtual workdays, and then compare differences in task preference, social frequency, meeting participation, growth path, and everyday role behavior. Students can also create multiple role profiles or observe different NPCs to compare the daily work of engineers, product managers, marketers, finance staff, HR staff, operations staff, and managers. Compared with static career descriptions, the system provides a dynamic and inspectable career-exploration process: students can see not only what a job description says, but also how a role acts, why it acts that way, which personality parameters affect its decisions, and how those behaviors gradually accumulate into task preferences, social relationships, and career-path changes. This supports classroom discussion, career-interest clarification, and role-awareness training by helping users understand the everyday behavioral differences among jobs and the match between personal traits and workplace environments.

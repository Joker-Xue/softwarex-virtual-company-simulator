### Response to Reviewer #2-5: Practical impact is not sufficiently concrete

Thank you for this helpful suggestion. We agree that the previous version did not describe the practical use of the system in sufficient detail, even though career exploration was mentioned as an intended application scenario.

In the revised manuscript, we expanded the description of practical impact by adding a **Preliminary User Study** section. This section clarifies both how the system is used and what kind of evidence was collected. Specifically, we recruited 26 university student volunteers who had not previously used the system. In the pre-test stage, participants completed an MBTI assessment, received three recommended career roles generated by the system, and rated the perceived suitability of each role using a 7-point Likert scale. In the experience stage, each participant entered the virtual company as one of the recommended roles and worked for at least three virtual workdays, during which they could freely interact with NPC agents, complete tasks, and participate in meetings. In the post-test stage, participants re-rated the three candidate roles and completed the System Usability Scale (SUS) and an open-ended feedback questionnaire.

We also added the corresponding pilot results to make the practical impact more concrete. After the virtual experience, participants' perceived suitability for the experienced recommended role increased significantly (+0.8, p < 0.01), while the change for recommended but unexperienced roles was not significant (p = 0.26). Their perceived suitability for non-recommended roles decreased significantly (-0.6, p < 0.05), suggesting that the simulation helped participants better distinguish between suitable and unsuitable career options. The SUS score was 72.3 on average (SD = 8.6), with 80.8% of participants scoring above the acceptable usability threshold of 68.

To avoid overstating the evidence, we describe this evaluation as a **small-scale pilot study** rather than a large-scale controlled user study. We further state in the conclusion that larger-scale studies are needed in future work to verify the system's impact on real career decision-making. These revisions make the deployment process, target users, system interaction flow, observed outcomes, and practical value of the system more explicit.
